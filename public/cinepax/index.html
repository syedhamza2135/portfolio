Where the money leaks

Three screens where people give up, and how they could work instead.

I booked a ticket on the real site, start to finish. These are the three screens that lost me. On the left is what happens today. On the right is one way it could work. The exact look is still open, that part is a conversation, but the direction is what matters.

01 / HOMEPAGE

The first thing every visitor sees looks broken.

Right now the homepage opens with a full-screen pop-up that makes you choose a cinema before you can see a single movie. It has no title, it carries a warning "!" icon as if something went wrong, there is no way to close it, and the OK button sits greyed out until you commit. It reads like an error, not a welcome.

02 / SEAT SELECTION

The seat map has no seats you can pick.

This is the moment someone has decided to buy. They go to pick a seat. Except none of the seats can be clicked, they have no numbers, the legend does not match the colours on the screen, and nothing shows where the screen even is. The site quietly assigns a seat for them. Most people assume the show is sold out and leave.

The bigger cost

You are renting your own customers from Bookme.

−%A commission on every ticket that sells through Bookme and the other resellers
−dataThe customer. Their email and their history sit with the reseller, not with you
−F&BThe popcorn. There is no food upsell on somebody else's checkout page
+ownYour own site, done well, keeps the margin, the data and the upsell in house

The site is hard enough to use that a big share of Cinepax bookings go through third-party aggregators instead. That is convenient for the customer, and it is expensive for you. Every one of those tickets is a commission paid, a customer you never actually meet, and a snack sale that never happens.

The big chains abroad, like AMC and Vue and Cineworld, win by keeping all of that in house. Live seat maps, food ordered ahead to the seat, saved payment, their own loyalty scheme. Cinepax is big enough to do the same. What is missing is the storefront.
